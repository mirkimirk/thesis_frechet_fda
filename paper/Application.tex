In this section, we show how the LQD method and Fréchet method work in practice and
compare their performance in a simulation study. First, we will describe the DGP that
will produce the data for our illustrations and describe the density estimation step.
Before turning to the results of the simulation study, we will use a sample dataset
generated by this DGP to illustrate the behavior of the two methods.

\subsection{Data Generating Process}
\label{sec:app_dgp}
The DGP used here is (almost) the same as \textcite{PetersenMüller2019} used in their first simulation
scenario. We generate a sample of qfs that is positively linearly dependent on our predictor
$p$. The basis is a normal qf whose location and scale both rise linearly in
$p$. The qf-valued stochastic process is given as follows:
\[
    m_{\oplus}(x)(\cdot) = \mathbb{E}[Y(\cdot)|X = x] = \mu_0 + \beta x + (\sigma_0 + \gamma x) \Phi^{-1}(\cdot),
\]
where $\Phi$ is the cdf of the standard normal distribution.
Compared to the version of the authors, we slightly exaggerated the parameters for more pronounced visualization. In our case
$\beta = 4$ and $\gamma = 2$, while having $\mu_0$ and $\sigma_0$ both still equal to 0 and 3, respectively.

The sample is generated as follows. First we sample the predictor $p \sim U([-1,1])$.
Then we draw the corresponding location and scale parameters as follows
\[
    \mu|X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0 + \beta X, v_1)
\]
\[
    \sigma|X \sim \text{Gam} \left( \frac{( \sigma_0 + \gamma X )^2}{v_2}, \frac{v_2}{\sigma_0 + \gamma X} \right).
\]
Sampling the scale from a Gamma distribution ensures drawing positive parameters, as is
required. The values for $v_1$ and $v_2$ are $0.25$ and $2$, respectively. Drawing $\mu$ and
$\sigma$ like this adds some noise to the observations: $\mu$ oscillates with standard
deviation $v_1$, and $\sigma$ with variance $v_2$, around the respective true values.

More details can be found in the paper. We now briefly mention the density estimation
step.

\subsection{Density Estimation}
\label{sec:app_density_estimation}
Since we will have a scenario where we have to estimate the densities $f_i$ from a finite sample
$x_{i1},\dots, x_{im}$ of observed points for each of the $f_i$. The densities were
estimated using a kernel density estimator with standard normal kernel. This gives
additional uncertainty and we will see in the simulation results if the estimates
by the different methods respond differently to this uncertainty though we do not expect them
to.

contained in the information of qdfs, so also not in the estimated LQDs or regression
coefficients.

more sophisticated estimation methods than linear interpolation to compute the left bounds
of the supports.

\subsection{Additional Scenarios}
\label{sec:app_additional_scenarios}
The DGP examined so far favors the Fréchet method in two distinct ways. First, the
true regression function is linear in the space of qfs, which is precisely the model
that global Fréchet regression postulates, so the Fréchet method is correctly
specified while the functional linear model in LQD space is not. Second, the supports
of the sampled densities differ, so the LQD method has to estimate the location of
the support, which, as discussed above, dominates its error. To disentangle these two
effects, we consider three additional scenarios, all in the setting of directly
observed densities and with $p \sim U([-1,1])$ as before. Each scenario was run with
200 Monte Carlo repetitions; the implementation can be found in the file
\textit{simulation\_new\_scenarios.py} in the repository.

In the second scenario, we remove the support estimation while keeping the truth
linear in the space of qfs. We perturb the uniform distribution on $[0,1]$ with
random transport maps \parencite[cf.][]{PanaretosZemel2016}: conditional on the
predictor, the response has the qf
\[
    Q(u) = u + a \, \frac{\sin(2 \pi u)}{2 \pi}, \qquad
    a = \alpha p + \varepsilon, \quad \varepsilon \sim U(-\nu, \nu),
\]
with $\alpha = 0.5$ and $\nu = 0.25$, so that all sampled maps are strictly
increasing and all densities share the common support $[0,1]$. Since
$\mathbb{E}[a \mid p] = \alpha p$, the true conditional Fréchet mean is again linear
in the space of qfs and the Fréchet method remains correctly specified. The LQD
method is misspecified --- the LQD of these densities,
$\log(1 + a\cos(2\pi u))$, is not linear in $a$ --- but no longer needs to estimate
the location of the support.

In the third scenario, we mirror the situation such that the functional regression
model after LQD transformation is the correctly specified one. We generate the LQDs
directly from the functional linear model
\[
    \psi_i(u) = p_i \cos(\pi u) + \varepsilon_i, \qquad
    \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.1^2),
\]
and obtain the qfs as $Q_i(u) = \int_0^u \exp\{\psi_i(s)\}\,ds$, so that all supports
start at zero. The true regression function is exactly linear in LQD space, while the
conditional qfs are nonlinear in the predictor, so now the Fréchet method is
misspecified.

The fourth scenario uses the location-scale process of the first scenario but makes
both parameters nonlinear in the predictor: the conditional location is
$4\sin(\pi p)$ and the conditional scale is $3 + 2p^2$, with the same noise structure
as before. Since both global Fréchet regression and the functional linear model in
LQD space are linear in the predictor, both methods are misspecified in this
scenario.

Table~\ref{tab:mean_ise_values_additional} shows the mean ISE values for the three
additional scenarios. The results confirm the interpretation of the first scenario.
In the common support scenario, the Fréchet method, being correctly specified, is
still more accurate, and its error vanishes at a fast rate with growing sample size.
The error of the LQD method, in contrast, settles at a low but constant level of
about $3 \times 10^{-4}$ --- its misspecification bias. The instructive comparison,
however, is with the first scenario: there, the mean ISE of the LQD method was
larger than that of the Fréchet method by a factor of almost 800 (at $n = 200$),
while here the factor is around 60 on an overall much smaller scale. Once the
common support removes the need to estimate the support location, the LQD method
becomes competitive, which confirms that almost all of its error in the first
scenario stems from the support estimation rather than from the regression step
itself.

In the third scenario the roles are exactly reversed. Now the error of the LQD
method decreases steadily with the sample size, as its regression model in the
transformed space is correctly specified, while the Fréchet method settles at a
misspecification bias of about $4 \times 10^{-3}$, roughly 45 times the LQD error
at $n = 200$. Notably, both methods remain accurate in absolute terms: the
misspecified Fréchet estimates are still proper distributions close to the truth,
whereas the misspecified LQD method in the second scenario at least required the
common support to achieve the same.

In the fourth scenario, where neither model is correct, both errors are large and
do not improve with the sample size, as they are dominated by bias. The Fréchet
method degrades much more gracefully: its mean ISE of about 7 is less than a
quarter of that of the LQD method, whose error remains at the same level as in the
first scenario since the support estimation again dominates. A natural remedy for
both methods in such settings would be a local regression variant, such as the
local Fréchet regression or the Nadaraya--Watson-type estimator considered by
\textcite{PetersenMüller2019}.
